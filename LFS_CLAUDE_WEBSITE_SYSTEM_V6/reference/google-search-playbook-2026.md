# Google Search Playbook for 2026

This document translates current Google guidance into an LFS operating playbook.

## 1. What is working right now in regular SEO

The current Google-first baseline is simple:
- make sure pages can be crawled and indexed
- provide clear textual content that explains what the page is about
- build helpful, reliable, people-first pages
- make pages easy to navigate and pleasant to use
- support the page with relevant structured data where appropriate
- use strong internal linking
- make business details accurate and complete
- create page-level value, not just site-level branding

## 2. Technical eligibility comes first

A page should not be treated as an SEO page if it fails the basics:
- Googlebot is not blocked
- the URL returns a working 200 status
- the page has indexable content
- the content is not only trapped in images or inaccessible interfaces
- the page is not incorrectly canonicalized or noindexed
- important content is not missing from the rendered page

Implication for LFS:
- check crawlability before content work
- fix indexing blockers before rewriting copy
- ensure the important value proposition is visible in text

## 3. Helpful content beats commodity content

Google's own documentation stresses:
- original information, research, or analysis
- substantial and complete descriptions
- content that adds value beyond obvious rewrites
- signals of experience, expertise, authoritativeness, and trustworthiness

Implication for LFS:
- service pages must not be empty stubs
- city/service pages must have real local usefulness
- copy must explain the service, the process, trust factors, and next action
- do not create dozens of thin pages just to target keywords

## 4. Page experience matters, but it is not the only thing

Good page experience includes:
- good Core Web Vitals
- secure HTTPS delivery
- strong mobile usability
- easy distinction between main content and distractions
- avoiding intrusive interstitials and ad clutter

Implication for LFS:
- design must stay clean
- pages should load fast and feel stable
- motion and effects must not harm usability or speed
- mobile spacing, tap targets, and CTA visibility matter

## 5. Internal linking still matters

Google explicitly calls out internal links as part of SEO best practices.

Implication for LFS:
- every major service page should be reachable through clear internal links
- city/service relationships should be linked intentionally
- homepages should pass users and crawlers toward high-value pages
- do not leave important pages orphaned

## 6. Structured data helps Google understand the business

Structured data is not a magic ranking button, but it helps Google understand entities and page content.

LFS default uses:
- Organization on the homepage
- LocalBusiness where relevant
- Service or other appropriate schema where appropriate
- FAQ only when the content truly exists on-page
- Review markup only when it complies with Google's requirements
- structured data must match visible content

## 7. Local SEO is still built on relevance, distance, and prominence

Google Business Profile guidance still centers local visibility on:
- relevance
- distance
- prominence

Implication for LFS:
- complete and accurate GBP data matters
- categories matter
- service and business details matter
- reviews and review responses matter
- photos and freshness matter
- the website should support the same local story as the profile

## 8. What LFS should optimize on-page

For each important page:
- clear topic and page purpose
- clear service explanation
- visible trust signals
- visible service area or geographic context where appropriate
- strong primary CTA
- strong supporting CTA
- readable structure
- useful supporting media
- relevant internal links
- structured data as appropriate
- useful unique copy, not filler

## 9. What LFS should avoid

Avoid:
- thin city pages with barely changed copy
- generic AI fluff
- over-explaining technical jargon to users
- keyword stuffing
- making every page chase every keyword
- heavy visual effects that hurt load or clarity
- relying on a single global page to rank for every service and city

## 10. How LFS should prioritize SEO fixes

Priority 1 — Technical blockers
- indexing
- crawl issues
- canonicals
- broken pages
- severe performance issues
- broken mobile experience

Priority 2 — Search relevance
- weak titles and headings
- weak service clarity
- weak service-area clarity
- missing internal links
- missing or weak structured data
- poor business details

Priority 3 — User and conversion quality
- unclear CTAs
- poor trust placement
- confusing navigation
- poor mobile readability
- weak contact paths

Priority 4 — Expansion
- new service pages
- city/service pages with real value
- FAQ or educational support content
- image and video improvements
